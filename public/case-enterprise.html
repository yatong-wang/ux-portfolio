Integrating Legacy Tools into Enterprise Software

Help government workers complete assessments 50% faster by embedding simplified workflows from legacy tools.

Role

Sole UX/UI Designer

Timeline

~10 Months (2024–2025)

Team

10+ person Agile team (product owners, business analyst, developers, QA engineers)

lock

Confidential Work

Client details, user data, and business metrics are anonymized or generalized. The focus is on my process: research, design decisions, and how I worked with teams.

01 / TL;DR

report

Problem

Government workers skipped conducting required assessments because the legacy tools were too complex.

The organization wanted to integrate the two tools into their existing enterprise platform with unified UI.

route

Method

  • Conducted multi-phase research with four stakeholder groups
  • Closely collaborated with subject matter experts
  • Designed around the less than 5% of the functionality users actually needed
trending_up

Outcome

  • 93% user satisfaction (wireframe usability testing)
  • 50% faster task completion (projected)*
  • 80% reduced training time (projected)*

*Estimation. The project got cancelled before production rollout.

05 / User Research

Multi-Phase User Research

  • conversationUser interviews (gov staff & expert users)
  • pollSurveys on tool usage and preferences
  • laptop_windowsUsability tests of the legacy tools to establish a baseline

Key Insight #1

Legacy tools were overkill

It's like handing an amateur a professional high-end digital camera, when all they need is the camera app on their phone.

cognition_2

It means:

Design around the 5% core functionalities that actually matters. Make gov staff feel less overwhelmed, by presenting only the necessary information they need to complete their assessment.

Key Insight #2

Gov staff relying on templates, contrary to what experts told me

Domain Experts & Trainers

Gov Staff (majority of users)

  • Sufficient domain knowledge
  • Used for complex edge case & difficult problems
  • Infrequent, low-volume usage
  • Limited domain knowledge
  • Used for common, repetitive scenarios
  • Seasonal, high-volume usage on similar tasks
“Templates are useless. It's faster to build from scratch.”
“Having to build from scratch every single time is tedious and unrealistic.”
clock_loader_20

It means:

Saving time is a big deal. Gov staff juggles a lot; allowing reuse of templates will make their life easier.

Key Insight #3

New hires were left to figure it out on their own

A generation of veteran staff retired and took their knowledge with them. New hires, most without a technical background, had no good way to pick up tools that were already hard to learn.

menu_book

It means:

Design primarily for the non-expert users. Focus on learnability of the system, but need to balance with efficiency.

06 / Information Architecture

Mapping overlapping data inputs and actions

To combine the two tools, I used OOUX methods to create an object map. It gives me the "bones" of the interface and interactions for simplifying the workflows.

07 / Solution

Reinventing the flow

Every week I worked alongside the enterprise software team and the legacy app scientists to pressure-test my decisions.

Design Decision #1

Tool Selection and Conditional Loading

Makes the UI less overwhelming and more straightforward.

  • checkCheckbox to select the tool(s) to use
  • checkTab content conditionally loaded based on selection

Design Decision #2

Contextual Auto-Population and Defaulting

Removes the hassle of hunting for the right data from multiple sources.

  • checkAuto-population from integrated enterprise database and API
  • checkDefault based on desired logic

Design Decision #3

Guided Data Entry

The workflow was boiled down to its core and became more learnable.

  • checkStepped data entry via tabbed navigation
  • checkProgressive disclosure to keep user focused and less overwhelmed

09 / Takeaways

Learning

What I learned

  • person_searchTradeoff between user convenience and expert expectations: We need to carefully evaluate experts' assumptions and balance them with user reality.
  • handshakeCo-design with experts is important for designing specialized tools. Experts can help you evaluate early concepts.

Retrospection

What I'd do differently

  • monitoringMeasure learnability and cognitive load, not just satisfaction. Knowing users liked it isn't the same as knowing they could actually learn it.
  • group_addMore stakeholder involvement in UX activities: specifically cognitive walkthroughs with domain experts, engineers, and product owners.
