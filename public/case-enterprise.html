03 / Business Context

Staff were avoiding the very tools meant to help them

Internal USDA users were required to run field assessments using two external legacy desktop tools. The tools received low satisfaction ratings in business surveys and were routinely skipped. Without proper assessments, the consequences fall on the land and on the agency's mission.

The Goal

Integrate the two legacy scientific tools into USDA's enterprise software — simplify the workflow without compromising scientific integrity.

04 / Framing Question

“How might we maintain scientific integrity while making the workflow usable for users who are not domain experts?”

06 / Research Insight #1

Enterprise users need less than 5% of the legacy tools' functionality

I ran independent multi-phase discovery research — user interviews, surveys, and usability tests of the legacy tools. The core finding: the tools were overkill. They were too scientific and too cumbersome for the users expected to run them day-to-day.

  • groups User interviews across stakeholder groups
  • poll Surveys on tool usage and pain points
  • bug_report Usability tests of the legacy tools

The Analogy

It's like handing an amateur a professional high-end digital camera when all they need is the camera app on their phone.

07 / Research Insight #2

On using templates — expert views vs. actual user reality

One core task involves logging structured data, which can be pre-filled from templates and adjusted afterward. While waiting for access to interview enterprise users, domain experts told me templates were useless. User research invalidated that assumption.

psychology

Domain Experts & Trainers

  • check More domain knowledge
  • check Used legacy tools for complex edge cases — often to help enterprise users
  • check Infrequent usage; low task volume
“It's faster to build from scratch.”
groups_2

Enterprise Users

  • check Limited domain knowledge
  • check Used legacy tools for common, recurring scenarios
  • check Seasonal, high-volume usage on repetitive tasks
“Having to build from scratch every single time is tedious and unrealistic.”

What It Meant for Design

Saving time was the priority — enterprise users juggle many responsibilities and process high volumes. Templates became a first-class feature in the new workflow, not an afterthought.

10 / Trade-offs

Designing around a work-in-progress design system

Our team was among the first to adopt the new enterprise design system while it was still being built. Two patterns we needed — used frequently and central to efficiency — didn't exist yet.

12 / Takeaways & Retrospection

lightbulb

Takeaways

Enterprise UX is systems thinking

  • checkAssumptions from experts ≠ user reality. Research revealed that what domain experts assumed users wanted was consistently different from what users actually did.
  • checkSimplification can preserve integrity — if done with domain experts, not around them.
  • checkStakeholder alignment is as critical as execution. Without it, even well-validated designs stall.
replay

Retrospection

What I'd do differently

  • checkMeasure learnability and cognitive load during testing, not just satisfaction. Satisfaction alone doesn't tell you whether a design is truly learnable.
  • checkMore stakeholder involvement in UX activities — specifically cognitive walkthroughs with domain experts, engineers, and product owners.
