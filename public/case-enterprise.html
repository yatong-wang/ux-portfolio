01 / TL;DR

report

Problem

Government workers skipped conducting required assessments because the legacy tools were too complex.

The organization wanted to integrate the two tools into their existing enterprise platform with unified UI.

route

Method

  • Conducted multi-phase research with four stakeholder groups
  • Closely collaborated with subject matter experts
  • Designed around the less than 5% of the functionality users actually needed
trending_up

Outcome

  • 93% user satisfaction (wireframe usability testing)
  • 50% faster task completion (projected)*
  • 80% reduced training time (projected)*

*Estimation. Project terminated before production rollout.

05 / User Research

Multi-Phase User Research

  • conversation User interviews (gov staff & expert users)
  • poll Surveys on tool usage and preferences
  • laptop_windows Usability tests of the legacy tools to establish a baseline

Key Insight #1

Legacy tools were overkill

It's like handing an amateur a professional high-end digital camera, when all they need is the camera app on their phone.

cognition_2

it means:

Design around the 5% core functionalities that actually matters. Make gov staff feel less overwhelmed, by presenting only the necessary information they need to complete their assessment.

Key Insight #2

Gov staff relying on templates, contrary to what experts told me

Domain Experts & Trainers

Gov Staff (majority of users)

  • Sufficient domain knowledge
  • Used for complex edge case & difficult problems
  • Infrequent, low-volume usage
  • Limited domain knowledge
  • Used for common, repetitive scenarios
  • Seasonal, high-volume usage on similar tasks
“Templates are useless. It's faster to build from scratch.”
“Having to build from scratch every single time is tedious and unrealistic.”
clock_loader_20

it means:

Saving time is a big deal. Gov staff juggles a lot; allowing reuse of templates will make their life easier.

Key Insight #3

New hires were left to figure it out on their own

A generation of veteran staff retired and took their knowledge with them. New hires, most without a technical background, had no good way to pick up tools that were already hard to learn.

menu_book

It means:

Design primarily for the non-expert users. Focus on learnability of the system, but need to balance with efficiency.

07 / Solution

Reinventing the flow

Every week I worked alongside the enterprise software team and the legacy app scientists to pressure-test my decisions.

Design Decision #1

Responsive Enabling for Selective Workflow

New design allows the users to get the work done in one place, while having the freedom to choose which tool(s) to use.

Contextual Auto-Population and Defaulting

09 / Trade-offs

Designing around a work-in-progress design system

Our team was among the first to adopt the new enterprise design system while it was still being built. Two patterns we needed — used frequently and central to efficiency — didn't exist yet.

build

Remediation

  • checkDesigned interim patterns with input from other designers
  • checkDocumented every design decision for traceability
  • checkFiled backlog requests with the design system team; followed up frequently
  • checkCommunicated scope changes to product owners; phased features into post-MVP releases
check_circle

Result

  • checkMaintained visual consistency across the platform
  • checkSurfaced cross-team dependencies early
  • checkContributed patterns back to the evolving design system

11 / Takeaways & Retrospection

lightbulb

Takeaways

Enterprise UX is systems thinking

  • checkAssumptions from experts ≠ user reality. Research revealed that what domain experts assumed users wanted was consistently different from what users actually did.
  • checkSimplification can preserve integrity — if done with domain experts, not around them.
  • checkStakeholder alignment is as critical as execution. Without it, even well-validated designs stall.
replay

Retrospection

What I'd do differently

  • checkMeasure learnability and cognitive load during testing, not just satisfaction. Satisfaction alone doesn't tell you whether a design is truly learnable.
  • checkMore stakeholder involvement in UX activities — specifically cognitive walkthroughs with domain experts, engineers, and product owners.
